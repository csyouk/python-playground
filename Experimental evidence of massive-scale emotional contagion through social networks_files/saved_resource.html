    Charles D. Claiborn • 4 days ago

    I am writing to express ethical concerns not only about the research by Kramer,
    Guillory, and Hancock, but about the “Editorial Expression of Concern” that
    PNAS published some days after the article appeared.

    First, the Expression of Concern seems to miss the point that the research was not about
    privacy per se, but specifically about informed consent in a situation that
    involved more than minimal risk to the human participants. The research
    involved an experimental manipulation of the news feeds of Facebook users, and
    the manipulation had the possibility of affecting the users’ emotions.
    Participants were not informed of the study and its risks in such a way as to
    allow them to opt out if they so chose. Indeed, they were not informed of the
    study at all. It is false of the authors to say, as they did in the article,
    that the study “was consistent with Facebook’s Data Use Policy, to which all
    users agree prior to creating an account on Facebook” and that this constituted
    “informed consent for this research.” Nothing in the Policy mentions the
    possibility of experimental research involving more than minimal risk. Users
    may agree for their data to be used in "research," but they do not
    specifically agree to be subject to experimental manipulations, because this
    possibility is not even mentioned in the Policy. Neither is the additional
    possibility that the procedures of the study might pose some harm.

    Second, according to the Expression of Concern, the IRB at Cornell University declined
    to review the ethical issues involved in the study “because it was conducted by
    Facebook, Inc. for internal purposes.” If this is the case, the Cornell IRB and
    PNAS were both in error. Two of the three authors of the study were affiliated
    with Cornell and subject to IRB review. In addition, the research was not “internal”
    at all, but was published in a scientific journal. It is absurd for PNAS to
    stress that it conforms to the Common Rule regarding informed consent, and then
    to say that it waives the rule if the research is conducted by a private
    company, no questions asked.

    In sum, the Expression of Concern does not go far enough. It should state firmly that
    informed consent procedures essential to the safe and respectful treatment of
    human participants were violated by the researchers in the study, that the
    violation was overlooked by PNAS.

                                                            Lee • 11 days ago

                                                            It's hard to know where to start here.

                                                            I think one place to begin is the unique situation commentary on this study is in. Anyone who used Facebook during the described time was potentially a subject in the study. My choice of the word “subject” here rather than the more politically correct “participant” is
                                                            intentional. The individuals included in this study were subjected to the study rather than participating in it of their own volition. This creates an interesting place for any commentary I have on the study. I am a researcher and have a set of professionally based opinions about the present study, but I may also be one of the subjects described in the study. For all the Facebook users, we are in a way a bunch of “schrodinger's research subjects”. No one knows if they were in the study or not. When PNAS hears the backlash against the study they should consider it in the light that some of the backlash is from potential subjects who are unhappy with their possible inclusion in a study that they did not ask to take part in.

                                                            Informed consent was the issue that first stood out to me about this research.

                                                            The authors state:
                                                            “As such, it was consistent with Facebook’s Data Use Policy, to which all users agree prior to creating an account on Facebook, constituting informed consent for this research”

                                                            There are points about the controversy in this study which can be debated, but I think the clearest issue is that in no way do the methods described here match any definition of informed consent.

                                                            If PNAS decides to take a bare minimum of action it should be to correct this inaccurate statement in the paper.

                                                            You can argue that maybe this study could have had been eligible for a waiver of informed consent. This is possible. The authors of this paper may very well have done everything within the legal rules. But the paper is not within the spirit of the ethical principals behind the rules. This paper seems to have worked it’s way through the system via a series of
                                                            loopholes. Facebook is not under the same ethical obligations of academia, so it conducted the research. The Cornell IRB then determined that they have no jurisdiction over the research. So then the paper came to PNAS and PNAS failed the one opportunity this work had for ethical scrutiny. What is puzzling to me is that the paper clearly does not meet the rules for HSR that PNAS outlines in their author policies. There was no IRB because Facebook conducted the research and does not have an IRB. The paper says there was informed consent (informed consent is a required journal policy) but the “informed consent” in the paper does not match any definition of informed consent that others might use. PNAS
                                                            policies clearly indicate that this work should not have been published, but for some reason they published it anyway.

                                                            There are still so many questions about this work that remain:
                                                            -What role did the Cornell authors have in design pre-collection?
                                                            -Why did PNAS ignore their journal policies?
                                                            -Were minors included in the study (the paper has a puzzling lack of demographic data)?
                                                            -What other similar work has Facebook done that is still in peer review somewhere?

                                                            One of my big concerns is that left unchecked, other work will appear that takes advantage of similar legal loopholes as are present in this study. By publishing the paper despite the violation of journal policies this seems to set a stage that may encourage more work wit similar flaws.

                                                            It is damaging to all researchers who work with human participants when studies take place which reduce public trust in the system. How many people do you think feel inspired to participate in psychological research after reading about this work?

                                                            I think people are well informed that Facebook lacks solid privacy values. But there is a huge distinction between engaging with Facebook with the knowledge that I place some privacy at risk and being an unwitting participant in experimental (aka non-observational) psychological research.

                                                            I worry that research without adequate informed consent will create distrust towards psychological research among the general public. Providing informed consent is a part of showing respect for the rights of participants who volunteer their time to help science. If people don't feel researchers have this respect for them, I worry it may make people less
                                                            comfortable with the idea of participating in research. The potential impact of condoning this work may have ripple effects that harm the field as a whole.

                                                                      Reza Yousefi Nooraie • 11 days ago

                                                                      Apart from the heated debate over the ethical appropriateness of the experiment, I believe the way the authors interpreted the findings is another matter of concern. These findings could be interpreted alternatively as a case of "giggling at funeral"! People tend to conform to the norms, and refrain from expressing themselves in a way that seems discordant to the context. If my friends are unhappy, I would tend to keep my ecstatic feelings to myself, and would hesitate to express extraordinary happiness, even though I am not as unhappy within. I think we should distinguish between the terms "mood" (as a pervasive and long term emotional state) and "affect" (as a temporary and immediate expression of emotions.) These findings seem to show how people moderate their affects in different emotional contexts, rather than how ego's mood is influenced by alter's. This claim is supported by the tiny effect size of 0.06-0.07% differences in probability of using emotionally charged words. For example, the study showed that if negative content is reduced in the news feeds, the probability of using negative words reduced by 0.07% (or 7 words out of 10000 words). This by no means represent a long-term and overall change in emotional state (i.e. mood). Consistently, I interpret the observed withdrawal effect (people who were exposed to less emotional content expressed less emotions) as the lack of motivation to express emotions when the context is not motivating, and not as the case of flat mood!

                                                                            James H. Fowler • 12 days ago

                                                                            I do not understand why the editors issued an expression of concern. They write: "the collection of the data by Facebook may have involved practices that were not fully consistent with the principles of obtaining informed consent." But this claim is false.

                                                                            This study was very clearly a 'minimal risk' study as defined by the Common Rule (http://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/humans...

                                                                            "Minimal risk means that the probability and magnitude of harm or discomfort anticipated in the research are not greater in and of themselves than those ordinarily encountered in daily life or during the performance of routine physical or psychological examinations or tests."

                                                                            As an example, a routine blood draw would be considered minimal risk.

                                                                            Since all this experiment did was to change the set of information shown in the Facebook newsfeed, and since Facebook users experience changes in the algorithm all the time, I think the only reasonable interpretation is that this study is minimal risk. There is no evidence that the researchers did anything that exceeds risks normally encountered on the Facebook platform.

                                                                            As such, it is eligible for waiver of consent, as indicated in the Common Rule. This is exactly how we obtained IRB approval from the University of California, San Diego, for similar experiments like Bond et al. 2012 published in Nature (http://fowler.ucsd.edu/massive.... It is also standard practice for nearly all large scale field experiments like get-out-the-vote studies. IRBs that understand the Common Rule typically allow these studies to be conducted WITHOUT consent.

                                                                            Therefore the Facebook experiment WAS fully consistent with the Common Rule and with the principles of obtaining informed consent.

                                                                            The Common Rule was influenced by hundreds of people who thought a lot about ethical issues in research. They understood that it is unreasonable to require subjects to be informed about every study. In cases of minimal risk, consent actually poses a net burden to subjects, creating harm where none existed before, and the generalizability of the results can be so skewed as to invalidate the whole exercise.

                                                                            I implore the PNAS editors to rethink their position on this issue. We already have a Common Rule that provides good protection for subjects, and I hope you take some time to better understand it.

                                                                            If academics cannot publish studies like these, it will not end experimentation. It will instead reduce transparency and the potential public good that results when everyone -- not just private companies -- better understand how large scale systems work.

                                                                                                                Michelle N. Meyer William Sparling • 11 days ago

                                                                                                                There are several incorrect statements of U.S. law in this post, many of which have been repeated in the media, to the detriment of accurate public understanding of what the federal regulations governing human subjects research do and do not govern and, within their sphere of governance, what they do and do not allow. Whatever one's views of the ethics of this study, no one is served by premising that discussion on incorrect assertions about the applicable law. (Note: the law in other countries where
                                                                                                                English-speaking Facebook users were subjects, and the ethics of the
                                                                                                                study both here and abroad, are separate matters, neither of which I discuss here.)

                                                                                                                To begin, it is not true that "The common rule does not apply to this case, as it can only be applied to US Government research activities." The Common Rule, including the requirement of IRB review, applies by law [including the terms of the federal regulations themselves and contractual agreements entered into between institutions and the federal government] much more broadly than that. Non-exempt human subjects research must legally comply with the Common Rule when it is: (1) conducted by any of 15 federal agencies and departments, (2) funded by any of those agencies and departments, (3) conducted as part of an FDA application to market a drug, device, or biological [technically not the Common Rule but the FDA's nearly identical regulations], or (4) conducted by a student or faculty member of a college or university that, in the process of at some point accepting a federal research grant for some research project, has agreed by contract with the federal government to submit to IRB review ALL human subjects research in which the institution is "engaged."

                                                                                                                Early rumors to the contrary notwithstanding, this research was neither conducted nor funded by the federal government. However, the two academic authors were during the time of the study affiliates of Cornell (see correction to the PNAS paper clarifying this), which is among those institutions that has agreed to extend IRB review to all human subjects research in which it, Cornell, is "engaged." When an institution is and is not "engaged in research" by virtue of their affiliates' participation in a research projects depends on what, exactly, those affiliates did. The regulations themselves do not define "engaged in research," but those in charge of overseeing those regulations have issued a guidance document on this point (which Cornell has adopted), the best interpretation of which is that an institution whose affiliates help design the protocol and write up the results for publication but do not obtain data about the subjects through intervention, do not obtain identifiable private information about the subjects, and do not consent subjects is NOT "engaged in research." Hence, Cornell's IRB was not required by the federal regulations (or, it would seem, by its own internal policy) to review this study. And Facebook is not subject to these regulations at all. As a result, U.S. law did not require any IRB review of this study. (Yes, PNAS has its own policy concerning IRB review and informed consent, but that's not a matter of law, which is what I'm focusing on here.)

                                                                                                                Moreover, despite what you imply, it is not the case -- and has never been the case since HHS first promulgated these regulations in 1981 (adopted by the 14 other federal agencies in 1991) -- that all human subjects research that is subject to IRB review must involve the informed consent of the subjects. Lots of important research cannot be done with fully informed consent. As a result, the Common Rule has an entire subsection, 45 CFR 46.116(c)-(d), devoted to explaining when an IRB may alter the elements of informed consent that are ordinarily required and when it may waive the informed consent requirements entirely. IRBs notoriously make different decisions about the acceptability of similar and even identical protocols, and the "minimal risk" criterion at issue in 46.116(d) is especially susceptible to different interpretations, but an IRB very plausibly could have determined that this study involved minimal risk and qualified for altered, if not waived, informed consent.

                                                                                                                Finally, although it is orthogonal to any discussion of the Facebook study, the poster below, technik1, is incorrect about how U.S. federal regulators and IRBs tend to treat blood draws. The agency in charge of overseeing the Common Rule once issued a helpful “IRB Guidebook” (see http://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/archiv... which specifically cites "the risk of drawing a small amount of blood from a healthy individual" as an example of “minimal risk” research. Although that IRB Guidebook was last updated in 1993, and readers today are warned that “[d]evelopments over the intervening years have made portions of the Guidebook information obsolete, while portions of the information remain valid,” most U.S. IRBs continue to interpret single (or “minimal”) blood draws in healthy adults, and even sometimes in children, as minimal risk. See, e.g., http://www.research.umn.edu/ir...
                                                                                                                https://healthcare.partners.or... and
                                                                                                                https://www.nwhealth.edu/insti....
                                                                                                                In 2013, the U.S. Presidential Commission for the Study of Bioethical Issues issued a report describing drawing blood in children as "minimal risk." See http://bioethics.gov/sites/def... (at p. 68).
